Literature Survey

As dogs increasingly take on the role of family members in households globally, the consequences of pet misidentification, abandonment, and delayed recovery remain persistent and deeply concerning. Traditional identification methods — tags, collars, and microchips — are vulnerable to damage, loss, tampering, or require invasive procedures and specialised readers. These shortcomings are most severe in low-resource or high-mobility environments where conventional systems fail to provide timely or accurate identification.

Biometric identification using a dog's nose print has emerged as a viable and promising alternative. Similar to human fingerprints, canine nose prints are unique and consistent over a dog's lifetime, making them ideal for secure, non-invasive identification. Early adoption was hindered by poor image quality, limited annotated datasets, and variability in capture conditions. Recent deep learning advances have substantially overcome these barriers, opening the door to smartphone-accessible, highly accurate nose-print recognition.

Beyond identification, a holistic review of the literature reveals critical unmet needs in pet welfare: no existing system bridges biometric identity with GPS-based lost-pet recovery, AI-guided ethical breeding, or community-driven cruelty detection. The survey below maps the key prior works this research builds upon and extends.

Related Works

"A Competitive Method for Dog Nose-print Re-identification"

Shen et al. — CVPR Biometrics Workshop, 2022

Proposed data augmentation, ensemble modelling, and hybrid loss functions for nose-print re-ID, achieving 86.7% AUC on a challenging benchmark dataset. Established that deep learning can overcome capture-condition variability at scale.

"Dog Nose Print Matching with Dual Global Descriptor Based on Contrastive Learning"

Li, Wang, Wu, Shi & Ma — CVPR Biometrics Workshop, 2022

Introduced a dual global descriptor framework trained with supervised contrastive learning that enhances feature discrimination even under low resolution or motion blur — directly relevant to real-world smartphone capture scenarios.

"DNNet: Dog Nose Print Pattern-Based Identification Using Deep Neural Networks"

Han, Park & Kim — IEEE Int. Conf. on Humanoid and Intelligent Systems, 2021

Demonstrated that CNN-based pattern extraction from nasal ridge, crease, and valley structures can achieve high identification accuracy, validating nose prints as a biometric modality comparable to human fingerprints.

"Development of a Mobile Application for Searching Lost Pets Using Geographical Location and Image Recognition"

Ortiz Mora, Salcedo Parra & Espitia R. — Int. Journal of Applied Engineering Research, 2018

Demonstrated how GPS coordinates and image recognition could be combined to improve lost-pet localisation in urban settings. The system remained limited in biometric precision and scalability, highlighting the gap this research fills.

Petnow & NOSEiD — Commercial Nose-print Applications

Commercial Products — 2020–present

Both apps enable nose-print capture via smartphone for pet registration and recovery. However, they operate in isolation — neither integrates breeding guidance, cruelty reporting, nor a welfare authority alerting pipeline, confirming the need for a unified platform.

AI-Based Animal Cruelty Detection Using Object Detection Models

Prior Works in Visual Cruelty & Welfare AI — 2020–2024

Emerging research applies YOLO and Faster R-CNN architectures to detect visible signs of animal abuse and neglect in images. No prior work, however, links detected cruelty evidence directly to a biometrically verified dog identity for traceability and repeat-offender tracking.

Research Gap

Despite significant advances in canine biometric identification and individual welfare tools, the literature reveals four critical gaps that this research addresses:

1. Narrow Identification Scope

Existing systems — Petnow, NOSEiD, and academic models by Shen et al. and Li et al. — focus solely on nose-print matching for identity verification. None extend this into a comprehensive welfare management lifecycle covering registration, recovery, breeding, and cruelty reporting.

2. No Ethical Breeding Integration

No current biometric or pet identification system integrates AI-driven breeding recommendations that analyse genetic diversity, health screenings, and hereditary risk. Breeding decisions remain manual, subjective, and disconnected from a dog's verified biometric profile.

3. Lack of Multi-Modal Biometric Recovery

Existing pet identification systems rely on single-modal methods. There is limited support for geolocation-based recovery workflows and no secure, privacy-focused communication channel for when owner matching fails, leaving finders and shelters without a cohesive reunification path.

4. Absence of Integrated Cruelty Detection

No existing solution links community-submitted visual evidence to identified dog profiles for cruelty tracking. There is no automated system that uses deep learning to classify abuse and neglect and dispatch alerts to welfare authorities based on a verified biometric identity.

Research Problem

"How can a non-invasive, AI-powered biometric system using dog nose prints be designed to provide accurate identification while integrating lost pet recovery, ethical breeding guidance, and community cruelty detection into a unified, privacy-preserving, and scalable welfare platform?"

Traditional pet identification methods — tags, collars, and microchips — are vulnerable to damage, loss, tampering, or require invasive procedures and specialised readers. These shortcomings are most severe in low-resource or high-mobility environments. While deep learning-based nose-print systems have demonstrated promising accuracy, no system integrates biometric identification with GPS-based recovery workflows, ethical breeding recommendations, and AI-powered cruelty detection in a single, smartphone-accessible, privacy-compliant platform.

This gap leaves pet owners, shelters, breeders, and welfare authorities without a cohesive, scalable tool for modern canine care. SnoutScan addresses this by proposing a unified platform that links a dog's biometric identity to every stage of its welfare lifecycle — from registration and recovery, to responsible breeding and community-driven cruelty intervention.

Research Objectives

Main Objective

Develop and evaluate an AI-powered platform for dog identification, recovery, and welfare monitoring using non-invasive biometric techniques — specifically nose-print recognition via smartphone — as a scalable, privacy-preserving, and animal-friendly alternative to traditional methods.

Specific Objectives

1

Nose-Print Biometric Identification

Implement a smartphone-based nose-print capture and recognition pipeline using CNNs and contrastive learning to generate tamper-proof biometric signatures linked to secure digital dog profiles — targeting 98–99% verification accuracy.

2

Lost & Found Recovery System

Develop a hybrid recovery workflow combining multi-modal image matching (face, profile, nose print) with GPS-based geolocation alerts to nearby users, shelters, and vets, and a secure in-app communication channel for verified identity coordination.

3

AI-Based Breeding Recommendation Engine

Build an AI-driven recommendation system that analyses genetic diversity, health screenings, breed compatibility, and breeding history to rank ethical pairing candidates, flag inbreeding risks, and simulate offspring health outcomes for responsible breeders.

4

Community Cruelty Detection System

Design an object-detection-based cruelty classification module that autonomously analyses community-submitted images to identify abuse and neglect indicators, links findings to biometrically verified dog profiles for cross-case traceability, and dispatches location-based alerts to welfare authorities.

Phase 1 — Requirements Analysis

Problem definition, target user identification (pet owners, shelters, breeders, welfare authorities), functional and non-functional requirement elicitation, and user survey design & data collection.

Phase 2 — System Architecture & Design

Overall system architecture design, UI/UX wireframes and navigation flows for the Flutter mobile app and React back-office, technology stack selection, and AI/ML model architecture planning (CNNs, transformer-based architectures, contrastive learning, object detection).

Phase 3 — Integration & Development

RESTful API development, PostgreSQL database integration, mobile-to-backend integration, ML model training pipelines using Python/FastAPI, and geolocation & alert service integration.

Phase 4 — Testing & Evaluation

Unit testing (frontend, backend, ML models), integration testing, model evaluation using MAE, Accuracy, and R² metrics, usability testing with target users, stress testing, and error-handling validation.

Phase 5 — Deployment

Mobile app deployment to app stores, backend and ML model serving deployment via FastAPI and Node.js, PostgreSQL cloud database hosting, and CI/CD pipeline configuration.
